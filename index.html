Factions & Fault Lines in Wikipedia's Admin Elections

Who gets to be a Wikipedia administrator — and who decides? We mapped 198,275 votes across a decade of elections to reveal the hidden factions shaping one of the internet's most powerful editorial communities.

01 — The Dataset

Wikipedia's Request for Adminship

Wikipedia administrators can delete pages, block users, and protect articles. To become one, editors must survive a public vote — the Request for Adminship (RfA). Anyone can vote, anyone can comment, and everything is on the record.

This project uses a dataset of every RfA vote cast between 2003 and 2013, published by Stanford's Network Analysis Project. Each record tells us who voted, who they voted on, whether they supported or opposed, the final outcome, and the comment they left explaining their decision.

Rather than just tallying votes, we asked a deeper question: do voters form factions? Are there groups of editors who systematically agree with each other — and groups who systematically oppose each other? To find out, we converted the raw voting records into a network connecting voters who cast ballots on the same candidates.

02 — The Network

Mapping the Voter Relationships

For every pair of editors who both voted on the same candidate, we asked: did they agree or disagree? Pairs who usually agreed got a positive connection; pairs who usually disagreed got a negative one. Only pairs with at least 4 shared votes were kept — filtering out one-off encounters.

This produced two networks. The agreement graph (5,106 voters, 498,430 connections) links editors who tend to vote the same way. The disagreement graph (4,317 voters, 105,995 connections) links those who persistently vote opposite each other. The average voter in the agreement graph is connected to nearly 200 others.

Degree distributions

Both networks are heavy-tailed — a small number of editors connect to hundreds or thousands of others, while most have only a handful of connections. The most-connected agreement hub has 2,691 connections; the top disagreement hub persistently opposes nearly 1,000 other voters.

Agreement strength

Agreement edges cluster strongly near 1.0 — when voters agree, they tend to agree consistently. Disagreement is more varied, suggesting different levels of ideological distance between opposing pairs.

The most influential voters

The top hubs in each network are almost entirely different people — the biggest consensus-builders are not the same as the biggest contrarians. These are structurally distinct roles in the community.

What each community talks about

Key insight The conflict is multipolar — communities 0, 2, 3, and 5 are all in tension with each other at significant scale. There is no single clear divide between two camps. The C0–C2 pair stands out with a conflict strength of 0.96: when members of these communities clash, they almost always land on opposite sides.
05 — Over Time

How the Admin Elections Changed

The dataset spans a decade during which Wikipedia transformed from a rapidly growing project to a more gatekept encyclopaedia. That shift is visible in the voting data.

Communities peaked in different eras

The six communities did not all rise and fall together. Community 5 — the largest — peaked in 2006, while Community 0 peaked as late as 2010. This raises a key question: are these genuine ideological factions, or just cohorts of editors who happened to be active at the same time?

The community became more selective

The proportion of support votes fell over the decade, and the fraction of candidates who were actually promoted dropped markedly — particularly after 2007. Becoming a Wikipedia admin grew significantly harder.

06 — Voter Stability

Did the Same Editors Keep Showing Up?

A faction is more meaningful if its members stick around year after year. We measured voter retention: for each year, what fraction of the prior year's voters came back?

Retention was high in the early years when the community was small and tight-knit, but fell as the voter base expanded rapidly in 2005–2006. By the late 2000s, large numbers of new voters joined each year while many veterans stopped participating — contributing to community fragmentation.

The most contested elections

Not all RfA elections were routine. Some candidates attracted fierce opposition. Some heavily-opposed candidates still made it through, reflecting Wikipedia's consensus-based rather than majority-vote process.

07 — Echo Chambers

Are the Communities Ideological Bubbles?

A true echo chamber is not just a group that agrees — its members form dense clusters of mutual agreement and rarely connect with outsiders. We measured this for each community.

Key insight Echo chamber strength is almost entirely a function of community size. The two tiny communities (C1 and C4, with 4 and 14 members) have near-perfect clustering and agreement — their members vote in complete lockstep. The four large communities cluster at a moderate ~0.5, making them loose coalitions rather than tight ideological bubbles.
08 — The Language of Opposition

Opposers Write More — A Lot More

Across all 198,275 votes, oppose comments average 44.8 words while support comments average just 19.3 words. Voters who reject a candidate feel compelled to justify themselves in detail; supporters often write just a few words ("Support per nom" is a common phrase).

This effect varies by community. Community 0 shows the largest gap — their oppose votes average 33 extra words compared to their support votes. The effect holds consistently across all communities, confirming it is a structural feature of the RfA process.

Curious finding Oppose comments on candidates who were ultimately promoted were longer (48 words) than oppose comments on candidates who failed (44 words). Voters opposing a successful candidate may feel they need to argue harder against the tide of community consensus.
09 — 2013 Snapshot

Do Factions Exist Within a Single Year Too?

One explanation for the six communities is simple: editors who were active at the same time naturally co-voted more, so the algorithm groups them by era rather than ideology. To test this, we ran the entire analysis again using only 2013 votes.

The 2013 subgraph contains 784 voters and 103,014 co-vote relationships. After filtering to agreement-only edges, the network has 783 nodes and 77,680 connections. Running community detection on this single year produced 9 communities — confirming that genuine factions exist within individual time periods, not just across the full decade.
